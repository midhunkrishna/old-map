## Why routes looked the way they did

Square-rigged ships could not sail closer than ~6 points (~67°) to the wind and made heavy leeway,
so ocean routes followed the planetary wind systems, not great circles:

- **NE trade winds** (~25°N to ~5°N) and the **westerlies** (~35–50°N) created the clockwise
  North Atlantic gyre: go south to go west, go north to go east.
- **SE trades and the doldrums** governed the South Atlantic; ships for Brazil or the Cape crossed
  the equator far west (sometimes within sight of Brazil) to avoid being becalmed off Africa.
- **Currents**: Gulf Stream/Florida Current (homeward boost, 2–4 kn), Agulhas (avoided westbound),
  Kuroshio (eastbound galleon boost), Canary and Equatorial currents (outbound boost).
- **Monsoons** in the Indian Ocean reversed seasonally and dictated departure windows entirely.

Sources: Ian K. Steele, *The English Atlantic 1675–1740* (OUP 1986), ch. 1–2; ocean-passage logic codified
later in M.F. Maury, *Sailing Directions*, and Ocean Passages for the World.

## 1. Outbound Europe → Caribbean ("the wheel," southern arm)

Channel/Biscay → Madeira (~2–3 weeks) or Canaries → run down to ~15–20°N to catch the NE trades →
landfall at Barbados or the Lesser Antilles arc (Dominica/Martinique passages for the Spanish) →
on to Jamaica/Hispaniola downwind. England→Barbados typically ~5–7 weeks (Steele).
Barbados was the standard first landfall precisely because it sits to *windward* of the whole
Caribbean — easy to reach, easy to leave from; hard to return to (hence "to windward").

**Season:** departures favored late autumn–spring; hurricane season (June–November, peaking
August–September) destroyed the 1715 and 1733 treasure fleets and disciplined all Caribbean scheduling.

## 2. Homeward Caribbean → Europe (northern arm)

Jamaica/Havana → beat through the **Florida Strait** riding the Gulf Stream → up the North American
coast to ~32–38°N → past or near **Bermuda** → eastward with the westerlies → **Azores** → Channel/Cádiz.
Jamaica→England ~10–11 weeks typical (Steele). This is why Bermuda, the Bahama Channel and the Azores
were strategic: every homeward-bound treasure ship and sugar ship funneled past them — and so did the
pirates of 1715–1726 (the Bahamas sat directly on the funnel's mouth, the core geographic fact behind
the Nassau pirate republic).

## 3. Carrera de Indias (Spain's convoy system)

- **Flota de Nueva España:** Cádiz (post-1717) → Canaries → Dominica/Guadeloupe passage → south coast of
  Hispaniola/Cuba → Veracruz (~2.5–3.5 months). Sailed ideally in spring/early summer.
- **Galeones de Tierra Firme:** Cádiz → Cartagena (~2 months) → Portobelo fair (Peruvian silver brought
  up from Callao via Panama). Increasingly irregular after 1700; fairs withering by 1730 under
  South Sea Company asiento competition and contraband.
- **Homeward:** both converged on **Havana**, then Bahama Channel → ~38°N → Azores → Cádiz (~2–3.5 months).
  The 1715 (combined fleet) and 1733 (flota) disasters both occurred on the Bahama Channel leg in
  late-July hurricanes — the schedule had slipped dangerously into hurricane season.

Sources: P. & H. Chaunu, *Séville et l'Atlantique*; A. García-Baquero, *Cádiz y el Atlántico 1717–1778*;
Carla Rahn Phillips, *The Treasure of the San José*.

## 4. Triangular trade legs (English/French slave trade)

1. **Outset:** Bristol/Liverpool/London or Nantes → Madeira/Canaries current → Guinea coast
   (Senegambia, Gold Coast, Bight of Benin, Angola). ~6–8 weeks to the Gold Coast; then often months
   trading along the coast.
2. **Middle Passage:** Gold Coast/Ouidah → Barbados/Jamaica/Saint-Domingue with the trades and
   Equatorial Current; early-18th-century means ~2 months (slavevoyages.org, Trans-Atlantic Slave
   Trade Database). Angola→Brazil was shorter (~35–50 days), one reason for Luanda's volume.
3. **Homeward:** sugar/rum via the Gulf Stream–Azores track as in §2.

This circuit is also the pirate hunting map of 1719–1722: Howell Davis and Bartholomew Roberts worked
the Guinea coast precisely because laden slavers and RAC ships concentrated there; Roberts died at
Cape Lopez (Gabon) in February 1722 fighting HMS Swallow.

## 5. VOC Brouwer route (Netherlands → Batavia)

Texel/Rammekens → (northabout Scotland in wartime, e.g., De Liefde 1711) → Cape Verde longitude →
SE trades crossed far west → **Cape Town** refreshment (~4 months out) → mandated since 1617:
run east in the **roaring forties (~36–42°S)** for ~1,000 Dutch miles → turn north → **Sunda Strait** →
Batavia (~8 months total on average; Bruijn/Gaastra/Schöffer, *Dutch-Asiatic Shipping*).
The turn north was dead reckoning with no way to fix longitude — overshooting wrecked the
*Zuytdorp* (1712) and *Zeewijk* (1727) on Western Australia.
**Season:** VOC sailings clustered in the Christmas, Easter and *kermis* (September fair) fleets.

## 6. EIC route to India

England (Downs) → Madeira/Cape Verde → wide SW sweep across the doldrums → round the Cape (often
calling St Helena homeward, not outward) → either up the Mozambique Channel or outside Madagascar →
catch the **SW monsoon (May–September)** to Bombay or round Ceylon to Madras/Bengal. ~5–6 months;
departures from England Dec–April were timed so ships reached the Indian Ocean during the SW monsoon;
homeward departures from India rode the NE monsoon (Oct–March).
Sources: K.N. Chaudhuri, *The Trading World of Asia and the English East India Company 1660–1760*;
Farrington, *Catalogue of East India Company Ships' Journals 1600–1834*.

## 7. Manila galleon

- **Westbound (easy):** Acapulco (departing ~Feb–April) → ~10–13°N straight downwind in the trades →
  Marianas (Guam) → Embocadero (San Bernardino Strait) → Manila. ~8–12 weeks.
- **Eastbound (dreadful):** Manila (departing June–July with the SW monsoon) → fight out of the
  Embocadero → north past Luzon to catch the **Kuroshio** → cross at **~35–40°N** with the westerlies
  (the route discovered by Urdaneta, 1565) → landfall near Cape Mendocino → coast down California →
  Acapulco (Dec–Jan). ~5–7+ months; scurvy mortality notorious. Up to half the year at sea made this
  the longest regular passage in the age of sail.

Source: W.L. Schurz, *The Manila Galleon* (1939).

## 8. The Pirate Round (c.1693–1723, residual by 1730)

North American/Caribbean ports (New York, Newport, Nassau) → Atlantic gyre south → round the Cape
well offshore → **Madagascar**: Île Sainte-Marie or St Augustine's Bay (refit, recruit, Baldridge's
post 1691–97) → north to the **Bab-el-Mandeb / Mocha** chokepoint or India's Malabar coast for the
August–September pilgrim ("Mocha") fleet → back to Madagascar to divide spoils → home via the Cape,
often selling out in the Caribbean or buying pardons (Bourbon amnesties of 1720s; La Buse hanged on
Bourbon, July 1730). Practitioners: Tew (1693), Every (1695), Kidd (1696–98), Halsey, England, Taylor,
Condent (Fiery Dragon, 1719–21).
Sources: Jan Rogozinski, *Honor Among Thieves*; Robert C. Ritchie, *Captain Kidd*; E.T. Fox.

## Speeds for the isochrone model

Documented/derived figures for period square-riggers (full-rigged ships, ~150–700 tons):

- **Burst speeds:** 8–12 kn for a clean ship running in the trades; rarely sustained.
- **Good day's run in the trades:** 100–150 nmi/day ≈ **4–6 kn** sustained.
- **Whole-passage averages** (port to port, including calms, beating, currents):
  - England→Barbados ~3,650 nmi in ~42 days ≈ **3.6 kn** average.
  - Boston→England eastbound ~2,900 nmi in ~32 days ≈ **3.8 kn**.
  - Westbound England→Boston the same distance in ~56 days ≈ **2.2 kn** — wind direction, not ship
    quality, dominates: build your isochrones *asymmetrically* by wind system.
  - Texel→Batavia ~13,500 nmi sailed in ~230–240 sea days ≈ **2.4 kn** overall (VOC averages, DAS).
  - Manila→Acapulco ~9,000+ nmi in ~180 days ≈ **2.1 kn**; Acapulco→Manila the same track area
    westbound ≈ **3.7 kn**.
- **CLIWOC** (Climatological Database for the World's Oceans, 1750–1850 logbooks — just after our
  period but the same technology) found fleet-wide mean speeds of roughly **4 kn**, with English and
  Dutch East Indiamen averaging ~4–5 kn while actually under way; consistent with the figures above
  once port delays and calms are folded in. (García-Herrera, Können, Wheeler et al., *Climatic Change* 73, 2005.)

**Modeling suggestion:** treat effective speed as a function of (route direction × wind system):
~4.5–5.5 kn downwind in trades, ~3.5–4.5 kn in westerlies with current help, ~1.5–2.5 kn for
beating legs (westbound North Atlantic, eastbound Manila, Mozambique Channel against monsoon),
plus fixed port/landfall penalties. That reproduces the documented passage times in `passages.json`
within ~10–15%.

## Seasonal calendar (quick reference)

| Window | Constraint |
|---|---|
| Jun–Nov (peak Aug–Sep) | Caribbean/Gulf hurricane season; treasure fleets that lingered (1715, 1733) died |
| May–Sep | SW monsoon: arrive India; impossible to leave Red Sea southbound |
| Oct–Mar | NE monsoon: leave India homeward; Red Sea/Mocha trading season |
| Aug–Sep | Mocha "pilgrim fleet" returns — Pirate Round ambush season at Bab-el-Mandeb |
| Dec–Apr | EIC departures from England; VOC Christmas/Easter fleets |
| Feb–Apr | Galleon leaves Acapulco; Jun–Jul galleon leaves Manila |
| Jul–Aug | Ideal (often missed) homeward window for Havana fleets, ahead of peak hurricanes |
