Concerning this Chart

This chart shows isochrones — lines of equal sailing time — for a well-found square-rigged ship of about 1730, computed from the port of your choosing.

The sea is modelled as a fine grid of ocean cells. A ship's speed over each cell follows the annual-mean wind belts of the age of sail (the NE and SE trade winds, the doldrums, the horse latitudes, the mid-latitude westerlies and the roaring forties), the major surface currents (Gulf Stream, Canary, Agulhas, Kuroshio and their kin), and the points of sail of a square-rigger, which made her best speed on a broad reach and could scarcely sail within seventy degrees of the wind. The fastest route between every pair of waters is then found by shortest-path search — so the chart, like a good sailing master, runs down the trades rather than beating straight into the wind. This is why the same crossing is weeks faster east-about than west-about, exactly as the record shows.

The model is calibrated against documented passage times of 1690–1740 — Steele's English Atlantic, Bruijn's Dutch-Asiatic Shipping, Chaunu's Seville records, Schurz's Manila Galleon, and the slavevoyages.org database. It represents a single well-handled ship with fair-weather routing: the great convoys (the Spanish flotas, the Manila galleon) ran slower than this chart by a quarter or more, as the table below shows.
